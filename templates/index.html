Frequently Asked Questions

  • What is liver cirrhosis?

    Liver cirrhosis is a late stage of scarring (fibrosis) of the liver caused by many forms of liver diseases and conditions, such as hepatitis and chronic alcoholism. It can lead to liver failure.

  • What are the symptoms of liver cirrhosis?

    Symptoms may include fatigue, weakness, loss of appetite, weight loss, nausea, abdominal pain or swelling, jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes), and easy bruising or bleeding.

  • What causes liver cirrhosis?

    Liver cirrhosis can be caused by chronic alcohol abuse, viral hepatitis (especially hepatitis B and C), fatty liver disease (non-alcoholic steatohepatitis, or NASH), autoimmune diseases affecting the liver, and certain genetic conditions.

  • How is liver cirrhosis diagnosed?

    Diagnosis typically involves a combination of medical history review, physical examination, blood tests to assess liver function, imaging studies (such as ultrasound or MRI), and sometimes a liver biopsy to confirm the extent of liver damage.

  • Can liver cirrhosis be treated?

    While liver cirrhosis is irreversible, treatment focuses on managing symptoms, preventing further liver damage, and addressing underlying causes. Treatments may include lifestyle changes (such as abstaining from alcohol), medications, and in some cases, liver transplantation.

  • What lifestyle changes can help manage liver cirrhosis?

    Lifestyle changes may include maintaining a healthy diet low in sodium and fat, avoiding alcohol and certain medications that can worsen liver damage, getting vaccinated against hepatitis A and B, and regular monitoring of liver function.
